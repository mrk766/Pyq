Business Studies — Complete Answer Sheet

1. Define partnership

A partnership is a legal form of business operation between two or more individuals who share management and profits. The partners are jointly and severally liable for the debts of the business, meaning personal assets can be at risk. It's governed by a partnership deed that outlines the terms and conditions.

2. What are MNC’s?

Multinational Corporations (MNCs) are large corporations that operate in their home country and also have facilities and assets in at least one other country. They have a global presence, significant financial resources, advanced technology, and powerful marketing, allowing them to influence economies and trade.

3. What do you mean by business etiquette?

Business etiquette is the set of customs and rules of professional behaviour in the workplace or in business dealings. It governs how people interact with each other, covering aspects like communication, attire, punctuality, and respect for colleagues and clients, ultimately reflecting the company's culture and professionalism.

4. What is internal correspondence?

Internal correspondence is written communication that takes place within an organization. It is not intended for the public. Common forms include memos, reports, emails between employees, circulars, and official notices. Its purpose is to direct, inform, or request action among staff and departments.

5. What do you mean by private limited company?

A Private Limited Company is a privately held business entity, where ownership is limited to a small number of shareholders. Its key features include limited liability for its members, restrictions on the transferability of shares, and a prohibition on inviting the public to subscribe to its shares or debentures.

6. What is meant by one-man ownership?

One-man ownership, or sole proprietorship, is a business owned, managed, and controlled by a single person. There is no legal distinction between the owner and the business. The owner is entitled to all profits and is personally responsible for all the business's debts and liabilities.

7. Define public ownership

Public ownership refers to the ownership of an enterprise, asset, or resource by the state or government on behalf of its citizens. These organizations, often called public sector undertakings (PSUs), are intended to provide essential services and promote social welfare rather than solely maximizing profit.

8. What do you mean by agency correspondence?

Agency correspondence refers to the formal written communication between a principal (a person or company) and their agent (an individual or firm authorized to act on their behalf). This correspondence typically deals with instructions, sales reports, commission statements, market updates, and other matters related to the agency agreement.

9. What is non-verbal communication?

Non-verbal communication is the transfer of information through the use of body language, gestures, facial expressions, eye contact, and posture, rather than words. It plays a crucial role in business by conveying attitude, emotion, and understanding, often complementing or even contradicting the spoken message.

10. What is business communication?

Business communication is the process of transmitting information and ideas both within and outside an organization to achieve its objectives. It encompasses a wide range of activities, from internal emails and reports to external marketing and public relations. Effective communication is vital for efficiency, productivity, and stakeholder relationships.

11. What are tenders?

A tender is a formal, structured invitation to suppliers to submit a competitive bid to supply goods or services. The process, known as tendering, allows an organization to select the most suitable and cost-effective supplier based on a set of predefined criteria, ensuring fairness and transparency in procurement.

12. What do you mean by extractive industry?

The extractive industry is a primary sector of the economy that involves the exploration, extraction, and processing of raw materials from the earth. This includes activities like mining for minerals, drilling for oil and natural gas, quarrying for stone, and forestry for timber. These materials are fundamental for manufacturing and construction.

13. State the objectives of a business organisation

  • Economic Objective: To earn profits for survival, growth, and expansion.
  • Social Objective: To provide quality products, create jobs, and contribute to community welfare.
  • Human Objective: To ensure the well-being and development of employees.
  • National Objective: To contribute to the nation's economic growth and follow the law of the land.

14. What are the limitations of sole proprietorship?

  • Limited Capital: The owner's personal funds and borrowing capacity are often insufficient for large-scale operations.
  • Unlimited Liability: The owner is personally responsible for all business debts, putting personal assets at risk.
  • Limited Managerial Skill: A single owner may not possess expertise in all areas of business (e.g., finance, marketing).
  • Lack of Continuity: The business's existence is tied to the owner's life and health.

15. Write a note on verbal communication

Verbal communication involves the use of spoken or written words to convey a message. It is the most common form of communication in business. Oral communication (meetings, phone calls) allows for immediate feedback, while written communication (emails, reports) provides a formal record. Clarity, conciseness, and appropriate tone are key to its effectiveness.

16. State the meaning and significance of business communication

Meaning: Business communication is the sharing of information, ideas, and instructions to promote an organization's goals.
Significance: It is the lifeblood of an organization. It facilitates planning, coordination, and decision-making. Good communication boosts employee morale, improves customer relations, and enhances the overall efficiency and public image of the business.

17. What are the objectives of public enterprises?

  • To promote rapid and balanced economic development in key sectors.
  • To provide essential services and infrastructure to the public.
  • To prevent the concentration of economic power and private monopolies.
  • To create employment opportunities and ensure social justice.
  • To generate revenue for the government.

18. Explain the significance of a cooperative organisation

A cooperative organisation is significant because it is a voluntary association of individuals who come together to promote their common economic interests. Its primary aim is service rather than profit. It operates on democratic principles ('one member, one vote'), protects its members from exploitation by middlemen, and fosters a sense of mutual help and self-reliance.

19. What are the different types of business communication?

  • Based on Channel: Verbal (Oral/Written) and Non-Verbal Communication.
  • Based on Organisation Structure: Formal (following official hierarchy) and Informal (grapevine) Communication.
  • Based on Direction: Downward (superior to subordinate), Upward (subordinate to superior), Horizontal (between peers), and Diagonal (across departments and levels).

10-Mark Questions

1. What do you mean by business correspondence? What are the different types of business correspondence?

Business correspondence refers to the formal written communication between organizations, or between an organization and its various stakeholders like customers, suppliers, investors, and government bodies. It is a critical tool for conducting business, building professional relationships, and maintaining an official record of all transactions and discussions. Effective business correspondence must be clear, concise, courteous, correct, and complete to achieve its purpose and uphold the company's reputation.

The different types of business correspondence can be broadly classified as follows:

  • Enquiry and Reply Letters: An enquiry letter is written by a potential buyer to a seller to request information about goods, services, prices, or terms. A reply letter provides the requested information and aims to convert the enquiry into a sale.
  • Order Letters: This is a formal letter sent by a buyer to a seller to place an order for goods. It must be precise, containing all necessary details like item description, quantity, quality, price, delivery address, and payment method.
  • Complaint and Adjustment Letters: A complaint letter is written by a customer to express dissatisfaction with a product or service. An adjustment letter is the reply, which aims to resolve the issue amicably by offering an apology, an explanation, and a solution (like a replacement or refund).
  • Sales Correspondence: This includes letters written with the primary objective of promoting and selling products. Examples are sales circulars, promotional offers, and follow-up letters to prospective customers.
  • Collection Letters: These are a series of letters sent by a seller to a customer to remind them of their overdue payments. The tone of these letters progresses from a gentle reminder to a more firm request for payment.
  • Circular Letters: A circular is a letter designed to convey the same information to a large number of people simultaneously, such as announcing a new product, a change of address, or a new policy.
  • Internal Correspondence (Memos): Memorandums or memos are used for communication within an organization. They are less formal than external letters and are used for making announcements, giving instructions, or reporting on activities.

2. Write a note on sole proprietorship. Explain its features and limitations.

A sole proprietorship is the simplest and oldest form of business organization, where a single individual owns, manages, and controls the entire business. From a legal standpoint, the business is not a separate entity from its owner. This means the owner is entitled to all the profits generated by the business but is also personally responsible for all of its debts and liabilities. It is the most popular business structure for small businesses, startups, and self-employed professionals due to its ease of formation and minimal regulatory burden.

Features of Sole Proprietorship:

  • Single Ownership and Control: The business is owned and controlled by one person, who has the final say in all decisions. This allows for quick and flexible decision-making.
  • No Separate Legal Entity: The law does not distinguish between the owner and the business. All contracts and business obligations are made in the owner's name.
  • Unlimited Liability: This is the most significant feature. If the business assets are insufficient to cover its debts, the owner's personal assets (like their home, car, or savings) can be legally seized to pay off the creditors.
  • Easy Formation and Closure: There are very few legal formalities or expenses involved in starting or closing a sole proprietorship. In many cases, it requires no formal registration.
  • Sole Beneficiary of Profits: The owner receives 100% of the profits and is not required to share them with anyone. This provides a strong incentive to work hard.
  • Secrecy: The proprietor is not legally required to publish the business's financial accounts, allowing for complete confidentiality in business operations.

Limitations of Sole Proprietorship:

  • Limited Financial Resources: The capital is limited to the owner’s personal savings and their ability to borrow money, which restricts the potential for significant growth and expansion.
  • Unlimited Liability: This is a major drawback, as it exposes the owner to significant personal financial risk. A business failure can lead to personal bankruptcy.
  • Limited Managerial Ability: One person cannot be an expert in all aspects of management (e.g., marketing, finance, production). This can lead to inefficient decision-making.
  • Lack of Continuity: The business's existence is entirely dependent on the owner. The death, illness, or retirement of the owner often leads to the closure of the business, making its lifespan uncertain.
